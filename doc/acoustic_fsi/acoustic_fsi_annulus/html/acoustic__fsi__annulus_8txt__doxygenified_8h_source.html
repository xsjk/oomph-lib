3\mainpage Time-harmonic acoustic fluid-structure interaction problems
4
5
6In this document we discuss the solution of
7time-harmonic acoustic fluid-structure interaction problems.
8 We start by reviewing the relevant theory and then
9present the solution of a simple model problem -- the sound radiation
10from an oscillating circular cylinder that is coated with a compressible
11elastic layer.
12
13This problem combines the problems discussed in the tutorials
14illustrating
15- <a href="../../../time_harmonic_linear_elasticity/elastic_annulus/html/index.html">the solution of the time-harmonic equations of linear elasticity</a>
16.
17and
18- <a href="../../../helmholtz/scattering/html/index.html">the Helmholtz
19equation.</a>
20.

24\section theory Theory: Time-harmonic acoustic fluid-structure interaction problems
25
26The figure below shows a sketch of a representative model problem:
27a circular cylinder is immersed in an inviscid compressible fluid and
28performs a prescribed two-dimensional harmonic oscillation of radian
29frequency \f$\omega\f$. The cylinder is coated with a compressible elastic
30layer. We wish to compute the displacement field in the elastic coating
31(assumed to be described by the <a href="../../../time_harmonic_linear_elasticity/elastic_annulus/html/index.html">equations of
32time-harmonic linear elasticity</a>) and the pressure distribution
33in the fluid (governed by
34<a href="../../../helmholtz/scattering/html/index.html">the Helmholtz
35equation</a>). The two sets of equations interact at the interface
36between fluid and solid: the fluid pressure exerts a traction
37onto the elastic layer, while the motion of the elastic layer
38drives the fluid motion via the non-penetration condition.
39
40\image html coated_pipe.gif "Sketch of the model problem: Forced oscillations of a circular cylinder (black) deform an elastic coating layer (pink) which is surrounded by a compressible fluid. "
41\image latex coated_pipe.eps "Sketch of the model problem: Forced oscillations of a circular cylinder (black) deform an elastic coating layer (pink) which is surrounded by a compressible fluid. " width=0.6\textwidth

43\subsection fluid The fluid model: the Helmholtz equation
44We describe the behaviour of the fluid in terms of the displacement
45field, \f$ d_i^*(x_j^*,t^*)\f$, of the fluid particles. As usual
46we employ index notation and the summation convention,
47and use asterisks to distinguish dimensional quantities from
48their non-dimensional equivalents. The fluid is inviscid
49and compressible, with a bulk modulus \f$ B \f$, such
50that the acoustic pressure is given by
51\f$ P^* = - B \ \partial d_j^*/\partial x_j^* \f$
52We assume that the fluid motion is irrotational and can be described by a
53displacement potential \f$\Phi^*\f$, such that \f$ d^*_j = \partial
54\Phi^*/\partial x_j^*. \f$ We consider steady-state time-harmonic
55oscillations and write the displacement potential and the pressure
56as \f$\Phi^*(x_j^*,t^*) =
57\mbox{Re} \{\phi^*(x_j^*) \exp(-{\rm i} \omega t^*)\} \f$ and
58\f$P^*(x_j^*,t^*) = \mbox{Re}\{p^*(x_j^*) \exp(-{\rm i} \omega
59t^*)\}\f$, respectively,
60where \f$\mbox{Re}\{\ldots \}\f$ denotes the real part.
61For small disturbances, the linearised Euler equation reveals that the
62time-harmonic pressure is related to the displacement potential
63via \f$ p^* = \rho_{\rm f} \omega^2 \phi^*\f$ where
64\f$ \rho_{\rm f} \f$ is the ambient fluid density.
65We non-dimensionalise all lengths
66on a problem-specific lengthscale \f$ {\cal L}\f$ (e.g. the
67outer radius of the coating layer) such that
68\f$x_j^* = {\cal L} x_j,\ d_j^* = {\cal L} d_j\f$ and \f$\phi^*
69= {\cal L}^2 \phi\f$. The non-dimensional displacement potential \f$\phi\f$
70is then governed by the Helmholtz equation
71\f[
72\frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial x_j^{2}} + k^2 \phi = 0,
73\ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (1)
74\f]
75where the square of the non-dimensional wavenumber,
76\f[
77k^2 = \frac{\rho_f (\omega {\cal L})^2}{B},
78\f]
79represents the ratio of the typical
80inertial fluid pressure induced by the wall oscillation to the
81`stiffness' of the fluid.
82
83\subsection elastic The solid model: the time harmonic equations of linear elasticity
84We model the coating layer as a linearly elastic solid, described
85in terms of a displacement field \f$U^*_i(x_j^*,t^*)\f$, with stress tensor
86\f[
87\tau_{ij}^* = E \left[ \frac{\nu}{(1+\nu)(1-2\nu)} \, \frac{\partial
88 U_k^*}{\partial x_k^*} \, \delta_{ij}
89+\frac{1}{2(1+\nu)}\left(
90\frac{\partial U_i^*}{\partial x_j^*}+
91\frac{\partial U_j^*}{\partial x_i^*}
92\right) \right],
93\f]
94where \f$E\f$ and \f$\nu\f$ are the material's Young's modulus and Poisson's
95ratio, respectively.
96As before, we assume a time-harmonic solution with frequency
97\f$\omega\f$ so that
98\f$U_i^*(x_j^*,t^*) = {\rm Re} \{ u_i^*(x_j^*) \exp(-{\rm i}\omega
99t^*)\}\f$,
100and we non-dimensionalise the displacements on \f${\cal L}\f$ and the
101stress on Young's modulus, \f$E\f$, so that \f$u_j^* = {\cal L} u_j\f$ and
102\f$\tau_{ij}^* = E \tau_{ij}\f$. The deformation of the elastic coating
103is then governed by the time-harmonic Navier-Lame equations
104\f[
105\frac{\partial \tau_{ij}}{\partial x_j} + \Omega^2 u_i = 0,
106\ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (2)
107\f]
108which depend (implicitly) on Poisson's ratio \f$\nu\f$, and on
109the (square of the) non-dimensional wavenumber
110\f[
111\Omega^2 = \frac{\rho_{\rm s} (\omega {\cal L})^2}{E},
112\f]
113where \f$ \rho_{\rm s} \f$ is the solid density.
114The parameter \f$ \Omega^2 \f$ represents the ratio of the typical
115inertial solid pressure induced by the wall oscillation to the
116stiffness of the elastic coating. We note that for a `light'
117coating we have \f$\Omega \ll 1\f$.
118
119
120\subsection bc Boundary conditions
121The inner surface of the elastic coating, \f$ \partial D_{\rm s} \f$,
122is subject to the prescribed
123displacement imposed by the oscillating cylinder. For instance, if the inner
124cylinder performs axisymmetric oscillations of non-dimensional
125amplitude \f$ \epsilon\f$, we have
126\f[
127\mbox{\bf u} = \epsilon \, \mbox{\bf e}_r
128\mbox{\ \ \ \ \ on $\partial D_{\rm s}$},
129\ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (3)
130\f]
131where \f$ \mbox{\bf e}_r \f$ is the unit vector in the radial direction.
132The fluid-loaded surface of the elastic coating, \f$ \partial D_{\rm
133f} \f$,
134is subject to the
135fluid pressure. The non-dimensional traction exerted by the
136fluid onto the solid (on the solid stress scale) is therefore given by
137\f[
138t_i^{\rm [solid]} = \tau_{ij}^{\rm [solid]} n_j = - \phi \ Q \ n_i
139\mbox{\ \ \ \ \ on $\partial D_{\rm f}$},
140\ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (4)
141\f]
142where the \f$n_i\f$ are the components of the outer unit normal on the solid
143boundary \f$\partial D_{\rm f}\f$ and
144\f[
145Q=\frac{\rho_{\rm f}({\cal L}\omega)^2}{E}
146\f]
147is the final non-dimensional parameter in the problem. It represents the
148ratio of the typical inertial fluid pressure induced by the wall
149oscillation to the stiffness of the elastic coating. The parameter \f$Q\f$
150therefore provides a measure of the strength of the fluid-structure
151interaction (FSI) in the sense that for \f$Q \to 0\f$
152the elastic coating does not `feel' the presence of the fluid.
153
154The fluid is forced by the normal displacement of the solid. Imposing
155the non-penetration condition \f$(d_j - u_j) n_j = 0\f$ on \f$\partial
156D_{\rm f}\f$ yields a Neumann condition for the displacement
157potential,
158\f[
159\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial n} = u_j n_j
160\mbox{\ \ \ \ \ on $\partial D_{\rm f}$}.
161\ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (5)
162\f]
163Finally, the displacement potential for the fluid must satisfy the
164Sommerfeld radiation condition
165\f[
166\lim_{r \to \infty} \sqrt{r}\left( \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial r} -
167 {\rm i} k \phi \right) = 0
168\ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (6)
169\f]
170which ensures that the oscillating cylinder does not generate any incoming
171waves.

176\section impl Implementation
177The implementation of the coupled problem follows the usual procedure
178for multi-domain problems in \c oomph-lib. We discretise the
179constituent single-physics problems using the existing single-physics
180elements, here \c oomph-lib's
181- <a href="../../../helmholtz/scattering/html/index.html">Helmholtz
182elements</a>
183.
184and
185- <a href="../../../time_harmonic_linear_elasticity/elastic_annulus/html/index.html">
186 time-harmonic linear elasticity elements</a>
187.
188for the discretisation of the PDEs (1) and
189(2), respectively.
190The displacement boundary condition (3) on the inner surface
191of the elastic coating is imposed as usual by pinning
192the relevant degrees of freedom, exactly as in a <a href="../../../time_harmonic_linear_elasticity/elastic_annulus/html/index.html">single-physics
193solid mechanics problem</a>. Similarly, the
194Sommerfeld radiation condition (6) on the outer boundary
195of the fluid domain can be imposed by any of the methods
196available for the solution of the single-physics
197Helmholtz equation, such as
198<a href="../../../helmholtz/scattering/html/index.html#ABCs">
199 approximate/absorbing boundary conditions (ABCs)</a>
200or a <a href="../../../helmholtz/scattering/html/index.html#DtN">
201Dirichlet-to-Neumann mapping.</a>
202
203The boundary conditions (4) and
204(5) at the fluid-solid interface are
205traction boundary conditions for the solid, and Neumann boundary
206conditions for the Helmholtz equation, respectively. In a single-physics problem
207we would impose such boundary conditions by attaching suitable \c FaceElements
208to the appropriate boundaries of the "bulk" elements, as shown in the
209sketch below: \c TimeHarmonicLinearElasticityTractionElements could be used to
210impose a (given) traction, \f$ {\bf t}_0\f$, onto the solid;
211\c HelmholtzFluxElements could be used to impose a (given) normal derivative,
212\f$ f_0\f$, on the displacement potential. Both \f$ {\bf t}_0\f$
213and \f$ f_0 \f$ would usually be specified in a user-defined namespace
214and accessed via function pointers as indicated in the right half of
215the sketch.
216
217\image html single_physics.gif "Sketch illustrating the imposition of flux and traction boundary conditions in single-physics problems. The continuous problems are shown on the left; the discretised ones on the right. "
218\image latex single_physics.eps "Sketch illustrating the imposition of flux and traction boundary conditions in single-physics problems. The continuous problems are shown on the left; the discretised ones on the right. " width=0.6\textwidth
219
220
221In the coupled problem, illustrated in the left half of the
222next sketch, the traction acting on the solid becomes a function
223of the displacement potential via the boundary condition
224(4), while the normal derivative of the
225displacement potential is given in terms of the solid displacement via
226equation (5). Note that corresponding points on the
227FSI boundary \f$ \partial D_{\rm f}\f$ are
228identified by matching values of the boundary
229coordinate \f$ \zeta \f$ which is assumed to be consistent between
230the two domains.
231
232The implementation of this interaction in the discretised problem
233is illustrated in the right half of the sketch: We replace the single-physics
234\c HelmholtzFluxElements by \c HelmholtzFluxFromNormalDisplacementBCElements,
235and the \c TimeHarmonicLinearElasticityTractionElements by
236\c TimeHarmonicLinElastLoadedByHelmholtzPressureBCElements.
237(Yes, we like to be verbose...).
238Both of these \c FaceElements are derived from the \c ElementWithExternalElement
239base class and can therefore store a pointer to an "external"
240element that provides the information required to impose the
241appropriate boundary condition. Thus, the \c
242HelmholtzFluxFromNormalDisplacementBCElements store pointers to
243the "adjacent" time-harmonic linear elasticity elements (from which
244they obtain the boundary displacement required for the imposition of
245(5)),
246while the \c TimeHarmonicLinElastLoadedByHelmholtzPressureBCElements
247store pointers to the "adjacent" Helmholtz elements that provide the
248value of the displacement potential required for the evaluation of
249(4).
250
251
252\image html multi_physics.gif "Sketch illustrating the imposition of flux and traction boundary conditions in the coupled multi-physics problem. The continuous problems are shown on the left; the discretised ones on the right. "
253\image latex multi_physics.eps "Sketch illustrating the imposition of flux and traction boundary conditions in the coupled multi-physics problem. The continuous problems are shown on the left; the discretised ones on the right. " width=0.6\textwidth
254
255The identification of the "adjacent" bulk elements can be performed using the
256\c Multi_domain_functions::setup_bulk_elements_adjacent_to_face_mesh(...)
257helper function. We note that, as suggested by the sketch above,
258this function does not require to the two adjacent meshes
259to have a consistent discretisation -- the identification of
260adjacent elements is based entirely on the (assumed to be
261consistent) boundary coordinate \f$ \zeta \f$ in the two meshes.
262We refer to
263<A HREF="../../../poisson/fish_poisson2/html/index.html#boundary_coords">
264another tutorial</a> for a discussion of how to set up (or change)
265the parametrisation of mesh boundaries by boundary coordinates.

567\subsection fsi Setting up the fluid-structure interaction
568The setup of the fluid-structure interaction requires
569the identification of the "bulk" Helmholtz elements that are
570adjacent to (the Gauss points of) the \c FaceElements
571that impose the FSI traction boundary condition (4),
572in terms of the displacement potential \f$ \phi \f$ computed by these

636\subsection ex Exercises
637- Equation (8) for the time-averaged radiated power
638 shows that \f$ \overline{\cal P}\f$ depends
639 on the derivatives of the displacement potential \f$ \phi \f$. This
640 implies that the value for \f$ \overline{\cal P}\f$ computed from the
641 finite-element solution for \f$ \phi \f$ is not
642 as accurate as the displacement potential itself. Computing
643 \f$ \overline{\cal P}\f$ to a certain tolerance (e.g. to
644 "graphical accuracy" as in the plot shown above) therefore tends to
645 require meshes that are much finer than would be required
646 if we were only interested in \f$ \phi \f$ itself.
647 \n\n
648 Investigate the accuracy of the computational predictions for \f$
649 \overline{\cal P}\f$ by:
650 \n\n
651 - increasing the spatial resolution e.g. by using
652 the command line flag \c --el_multiplier (which controls the number
653 of elements in the mesh) and suppressing any automatic (un)refinement
654 by setting the maximum number of adaptations to zero
655 using the \c --max_adapt command line flag.
656 \n\n
657 - reducing the outer radius of the computational domain, using
658 the command line flag \c --outer_radius, say.
659 \n\n
660 - varying the element type, from the bi-linear
661 \c RefineableQHelmholtzElement<2,2>
662 to the bi-cubic \c RefineableQHelmholtzElement<2,4>, say.
663 .
664 \n\n
665 Which of these approaches gives you the "most accuracy" for a given number of
666 degrees of freedom?
