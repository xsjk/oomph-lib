3\mainpage Motion of an elliptical particle in shear flow: Jeffery orbits
4
5 We study the problem of the motion of a rigid elliptical particle
6freely suspended in a shear flow as described by Jeffery (1922)
7[The motion of elliptical particles immersed in viscous fluid, <I>
8 Proc. Roy. Soc. A </I> <B> 102 </B> 161-179].
9 The problem is solved using \c oomph-lib's
10<a href="../../../../doc/meshes/mesh_from_inline_triangle/html/index.html">
11inline unstructured mesh generation </a> procedures to modify
12the fluid mesh in response to changes in orientation of the ellipse.
13
14\section overview Overview of the problem
15
16\image html jeff_sketch.gif "A rigid ellipse immersed in a shear flow "
17\image latex jeff_sketch.eps "A rigid ellipse immersed in a shear flow " width=0.75\textwidth
18
19We consider an ellipse with centre of mass fixed at the origin of a
20Cartesian coordinate system \f$ (x_{1}^{*}, x_{2}^{*}) \f$, but
21immersed in a viscous fluid undergoing a linear shear flow
22with shear rate \f$ G \f$. In the absence of the ellipse
23the fluid velocity field would be
24 \f$ u^{*}_{1} = Gx^{*}_{2} \f$, \f$ u^{*}_{2} = 0 \f$, where
25 \f$ u^{*}_{i} \f$ is the dimensional velocity component in the
26 \f$ x^{*}_{i} \f$ coordinate direction.
27
28 The configuration of a rigid body in two dimensions is determined entirely
29 by the position of its centre of mass, \f$(X^{*}_{1},X^{*}_{2})\f$
30 and an angle, \f$\chi\f$ that specifies its orientation to a fixed
31 axis. The equations governing the motion of the particle are then simply
32 conservation of linear and angular momentum:
33<center>
34\f[ M \frac{\mbox{d}^{2} X^{*}_{i}}{\mbox{d} t^{*2}} = F^{*}_{i} \quad
35 \mbox{and} \quad I \frac{\mbox{d}^{2} \chi}{\mbox{d} t^{*2}} = T^{*}, \f]
36</center>
37 where \f$M\f$ is the mass of the body, \f$I\f$ is its moment of
38 inertia about the centre of mass, \f$(F^{*}_{1},F^{*}_{2})\f$ is the
39 resultant force on the body and \f$T^{*}\f$ is the resultant torque about
40 the centre of mass.
41
42 In the present context, the force and torque on the body are entirely
43 due to the viscous fluid loading on its surface in which case
44<center>
45\f[ F^{*}_{i} = \int \tau^{*}_{ij}\, n_{j}\, \mbox{d} s \quad\mbox{and}\quad
46 T^{*} = \oint (x^{*}_{1} - X^{*}_{1}) \tau^{*}_{2j}\,n_{j} -
47 (x^{*}_{2} - X^{*}_{2}) \tau^{*}_{1j}\,n_{j}\, \mbox{d}
48 s, \f]
49</center>
50 where the integral is around the perimeter of the ellipse,
51 \f$\tau^{*}_{ij}\f$ is the fluid stress tensor and
52 \f$(n_{1},n_{2})\f$ is the unit normal to the ellipse surface, directed
53 away from the solid body.
54
55 We non-dimensionalise the rigid-body equations, using the same
56problem-specific reference quantities as used in the
57non-dimensionalisation of the Navier--Stokes equations, described
58<a href="../../../../doc/navier_stokes/driven_cavity/html/index.html">
59in another tutorial. </a> Thus, \f$ {\cal U}\f$ is a typical fluid velocity,
60\f$ {\cal L} \f$ is the length scale, \f${\cal T}\f$ is the time scale
61and the fluid pressure is non-dimensionalised on the viscous scale, \f$
62\mu_{ref} {\cal U}/{\cal L} \f$, where \f$ \mu_{ref} \f$ is the
63reference fluid viscosity. Hence,
64<center>
65\f[
66\tau^{*}_{ij} = \frac{\mu_{ref} {\cal U}}{{\cal L}} \, \tau_{ij}, \qquad
67x_i^{*} = {\cal L}\, x_i, \qquad
68X_i^* = {\cal L}\, X_i, \qquad
69t^* = {\cal T}\, t.
70\f]
71</center>
72The external forces and torques are non-dimensionalised on the
73viscous scales per unit length, \f$ F^{*}_{i} = \mu_{ref} {\cal U}
74F_{i} \f$ and \f$ T^{*} = \mu_{ref} {\cal U} {\cal L} T \f$.
75The dimensionless rigid-body equations are then
76<center>
77\f[ Re St^{2} \frac{\rho_{s}}{\rho_{f}}\, \bar{M}\,
78\frac{\mbox{d}^{2} X_{i}}{\mbox{d} t^{2}} = F_{i}
79\quad
80 \mbox{and} \quad Re St^{2} \frac{\rho_{s}}{\rho_{f}}\, \bar{I}\,
81 \frac{\mbox{d}^{2} \chi}{\mbox{d} t^{2}} = T, \f]
82</center>
83 where the dimensionless parameters
84<center>
85\f[ Re = \frac{\rho_{f} {\cal U}{\cal L}}{\mu_{ref}}, \quad
86 St = \frac{\cal L}{{\cal U}{\cal T}},\quad
87 \frac{\rho_{s}}{\rho_{f}}, \quad
88 \bar{M} = \frac{M}{\rho_{s} {\cal L}^{2}}, \quad
89 \bar{I} = \frac{I}{\rho_{s} {\cal L}^{4}}, \f]
90</center>
91are the Reynolds number, the Strouhal number, the density ratio, and the
92dimensionless mass and moment of inertia, respectively. In the
93above \f$ \rho_{f} \f$ is the fluid density and \f$ \rho_{s} \f$ is
94the solid density.
95
96 In the specific problem considered here, the centre of mass is
97 fixed, and the only possible motion of the particle is free
98 rotation. The particle motion is therefore reduced to the solution of
99 a single equation for the unknown angle. We choose \f$ {\cal L} = 2b
100 \f$, the major axis of the ellipse, \f$ {\cal U} = 2Gb \f$ and \f$
101 {\cal T} = G^{-1} \f$, so that \f$ St = 1 \f$ and the governing
102 equations for the fluid and solid become
103 <center>
104 \f[ Re \left (\frac{\partial u_i}{\partial t} +
105u_j \frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_j} \right) =
106- \frac{\partial p}{\partial x_i} +
107\frac{\partial }{\partial x_j} \left[
108\frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_j} +
109\frac{\partial u_j}{\partial x_i} \right]
110\quad\mbox{and}\quad
111\frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_i} = 0.
112\f]
113</center>
114 and
115<center>
116\f[ Re \left(\frac{\rho_{s}}{\rho_{f}}\right) \bar{I}
117= \oint \left[x_{1}\tau_{2j}\,n_{j} -
118 x_{2} \tau_{1j}\,n_{j}\, \right] \mbox{d} s, \f].
119</center>
120
121We perform the computations in the domain \f$ x_{1} \in [-L,
122 L]\f$ and \f$ x_{2} \in [-H, H] \f$, and apply the boundary conditions
123<center>
124 \f[ u_{1} = H f(t), u_{2} = 0, \quad\mbox{on}\quad x_{2} = -H; \f]
125 \f[ u_{1} = -H f(t), u_{2} = 0, \quad\mbox{on}\quad x_{2} = H; \f]
126 \f[ \tau_{11} = 0, u_{2} = 0, \quad\mbox{on}\quad x_{1} = -L, L; \f]
127 where \f$f(t)\f$ is a smooth ramp function such that \f$f(0) = \dot{f}(0) =
128 \ddot{f}(0) = 0\f$ and \f$f(t) \to 1\f$ as \f$ t \to \infty\f$.
129</center>
130
131\section results Results
132
133 Jeffery (1922) showed that for a two-dimensional ellipse in Stokes
134 flow (\f$ Re = 0 \f$), the exact solution for the angle as a function
135 of time is
136 \f[ \chi = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{b}{a}\tan \frac{abGt}{a^{2} +
137 b^{2}}\right),\quad\mbox{and}\quad \dot{\chi} = \frac{G}{a^{2} +
138 b^{2}}\left(b^{2}\cos^{2}\chi + a^{2}\sin^{2}\chi\right). \f]
139 Thus, the ellipse performs periodic orbits but with a non-uniform
140 velocity. For sufficiently small \f$ Re \f$, Ding \& Aidun (2000)
141 [The dynamics and scaling law for particles suspended in shear flow
142 with inertia, <I> J. Fluid Mech. </I> <B> 423 </B> 317-344]
143 showed that the system approximates the Jeffery orbits but with an
144 increased period. Typical solutions for \f$ Re = 1\f$ are shown
145 below.

154\section fsi Implementation as a fluid-structure interaction problem
155
156 The problem is a fluid-structure interaction problem in
157which the structural dynamics are particularly simple, depending only
158on a single degree of freedom.
159Nonetheless, the two types of physical coupling between the fluid
160and the solid remain:
161-# The position of the free boundary depends on the position
162 of the rigid body.
163-# The rigid body is loaded by the fluid traction.
164.
165
166 As in other
167<a href="../../../../doc/interaction/unstructured_fsi/html/index.html">
168 2D unstructured FSI </a> problems, we treat the fluid mesh as a
169 pseudo-solid body and determine the position of the boundary nodes
170 on the fluid-solid interface using
171 \c ImposeDisplacementByLagrangeMultiplierElements.
172
173 The rigid body mechanics is handled by using a \c GeomObject that
174represents the perimeter of the rigid body to create
175an \c ImmersedRigidBodyElement
176that solves the three equations of motion for
177a rigid body; and the load is applied to the rigid body
178using \c NavierStokesSurfaceDragTorqueElements.

207\section driver The driver code
208
209 After parsing the command-line arguments, which are used to modify
210 certain parameters for validation runs, a single instance of the
211 \c UnstructuredImmersedEllipseProblem (described below) is
212 constructed using Taylor Hood elements.
213
214\dontinclude jeffery_orbit.cc
215\skipline start_of_main
216\until problem;
217
218 After construction the \c Nodes on the boundary of the ellipse will
219 have been directly mapped onto the curvilinear
220 surface using a strong (collocation)
221 condition, \f$ x_{i} = R_{i}\f$, where \f$R_{i}\f$ is the
222 corresponding boundary of the ellipse.
223 In the full problem
224 the displacement boundary condition is enforced weakly
225 via Lagrange multipliers
226 \f$ \oint \left\{x_{i} - R_{i}\right\}\psi \mbox{d} s =
227 0\f$. In order to ensure consistency, we initially solve the problem
228 in which the rigid body is pinned so that the boundary nodes are
229 adjusted to be consistent with the weak form of the boundary
230 condition. We note that for sufficiently fine initial meshes
231 the difference is minimal.
232
233 \until solve_for_consistent
234
235 Now that we have a consistent initial condition, we initialise the
236 timestepper and set conditions consistent with a impulsive start from
237 rest.
238
239 \until doc_solution();
240
241 We then take a fixed number of timesteps on the initial mesh,
242 documenting the solution after each solve.
243
244 \until }
245
246 Finally, we loop over a number of ``cycles'' in which we adapt the problem
247 and then solve for a fixed number of time steps on the each mesh.
248
249 \skipline for
250 \until //end of main

254\section problem The Problem class
255
256 The Problem class follows the usual pattern. The time-dependent
257 boundary conditions are applied using the \c
258 actions_before_implicit_timestep() function and the no-slip boundary
259 condition is applied in \c actions_before_newton_convergence_check()
260 via an auxiliary node update function.
261
262 <a href="../../../../doc/meshes/mesh_from_inline_triangle/html/index.html#adapt">
263 Recall</a> that when adapting an unstructured mesh, its constituent
264 elements are completely re-generated. Physical
265 parameters and boundary conditions must therefore be reassigned, which is the
266 task of the \c complete_problem_setup() function, called in \c
267 actions_after_adapt().
268 Helper functions are also provided to solve the initial problem to
269 move the boundary nodes [\c solve_for_consistent_nodal_positions()];
270 to apply the boundary conditions [\c set_boundary_velocity()];
271 and to construct and delete the surface
272 elements that impose the Lagrange multiplier constraints and compute the load
273 on the rigid body [\c create_lagrange_multiplier_elements(), \c
274 delete_lagrange_multiplier_elements(), \c create_drag_elements(),
275 \c delete_drag_elements()].
276
277 The class also provides storage for the meshes, the rigid body
278and file handles for documentation.
279

281\skipline start_of_problem_class
282\until end_of_problem_class

286\section constructor The Problem Constructor
287
288 We begin by opening the output files and allocating two time
289 steppers, one for the fluid problem and one for the rigid body
290 problem.
291
292\skipline start_constructor
293\until Newmark
294
295 We then define the geometry that defines the outer boundary of the
296 unstructured mesh by constructing a \c TriangleMeshPolygon that
297 consists of four straight-line boundaries.
298
299\until new TriangleMeshPolygon
300
301 Next we build the single \c ImmersedRigidBodyElement from an
302 instantiation of a \c GeneralEllipse
303 geometric object.
304
305\until this->time_stepper_pt(1)
306
307 The \c ImmersedRigidBodyElement is used to define a
308\c TriangleMeshCurvilinearClosedCurve in exactly the same way as if it
309were simply a (passive) \c GeomObject, as discussed in
310<a href="../../../../doc/meshes/mesh_from_inline_triangle/html/index.html">
311another tutorial. </a>
312
313\until linear_boundary_pt,hole_
314
315 We then build the unstructured fluid mesh using the boundary
316 information, set a spatial error estimator and complete the setup of
317 the problem
318
319\until complete_problem_setup()
320
321 The \c ImmersedRigidBodyElement is not deleted during the adaptation
322 process and so its physical parameters can be set once in the
323 constructor. We set the initial position of the centre of mass,
324 as well as the non-dimensional mass and moment of inertia shape, the
325 Reynolds and Strouhal numbers, and the density ratio, which appear in the
326 governing equations above. For this problem, we also fix the location
327 of the centre of mass. (The section \ref comments contains an
328 exercise that asks you to explore what happens when you omit this step).
329
330\until pin_centre_of_mass_coordinate(1)
331
332 For later reference, we store the single \c ImmersedRigidBodyElement in a mesh
333
334\until add_element_pt
335
336 We then create the elements that apply the load on the rigid body and
337 pass the entire mesh of elements to the rigid body. This is the
338 equivalent of the function
339 \c FSI_functions::setup_fluid_load_info_for_solid_elements(..), but
340 here the procedure is very simple because <b> all </b> the surface
341 elements affect the single rigid body.
342
343\until rigid_element1_pt
344
345 We next create the mesh of Lagrange-multiplier elements that drive
346the deformation of the fluid mesh in response to the motion of the
347ellipse
348
349 \until create_lagrange
350
351 and then construct the global mesh and assign equation numbers.
352
353 \until end_of_constructor
354
355 Note that the \c Drag_mesh_pt does not need to be added as a sub-mesh
356 because its elements do not contribute <i> directly </i> to the
357 residuals and Jacobian.

362\section complete Completing the problem setup
363
364 The helper function \c complete_problem_setup() starts by
365 (re-)applying the boundary conditions by pinning the fluid velocity
366 in the \f$ x_{2}\f$-direction on all boundaries and that in the \f$
367 x_{1} \f$-direction on the top and bottom (boundaries 1 and 3).
368
369\dontinclude jeffery_orbit.cc
370\skipline start_complete_problem_setup
371\until ->pin(1)
372
373 The boundary conditions for the solid degrees of freedom that
374 describe the mesh deformation are assigned next by pinning the
375 nodal positions on the fixed domain boundaries (boundaries 0, 1, 2,
376 3):
377
378 \until }
379
380 The nodes on the boundary of the rigid body should be free to move,
381 so they are unpinned and an auxiliary node update function is set
382 to apply the no-slip boundary condition from the \c Node's positional
383 history values.
384
385\until // End loop over boundaries
386
387 We then loop over the fluid elements and set pointers to the physical
388 parameters and the apply the velocity boundary conditions
389
390\until end_of_complete_problem_setup

395\section surface Creating and destroying the surface elements
396
397 The general
398 procedure for creating, attaching and deleting\c FaceElements is
399 exactly the same as described
400<a href="../../../../doc/poisson/two_d_poisson_flux_bc/html/index.html">
401 in another tutorial, </a> so is not described in detail here.
402
403 The functions \c create_lagrange_multiplier_elements() and \c
404 create_drag_elements() construct surface
405\c ImposeDisplacementByLagrangeMulitiplierElements and
406\c NavierStokesSurfaceDragTorqueElements, respectively, around the
407 boundary of the rigid body, setting any required member data. For
408 example, the \c NavierStokesSurfaceDragTorqueElements require the
409 location of the centre of mass in order to compute the torque. The
410 elements are added to the internal storage containers
411\c Lagrange_multiplier_mesh_pt and \c Drag_mesh_pt.
412 The corresponding functions \c delete_lagrange_multiplier_elements()
413 and \c delete_drag_elements() are used to delete and remove
414 the elements before adaptation.

420\section bc Setting the boundary velocity
421
422 The function \c set_boundary_velocity() is used to apply the
423 time-dependent boundary conditions to the external boundaries of the
424 fluid domain. The only subtlety is that after a remesh the history
425 values for the boundary nodes must also be (re-)applied; and, for
426 simplicity, the history values are always reset.

431\section initial Solving for consistent initial nodal positions
432
433 The initial nodal positions are made consistent with the
434 weakly-imposed displacement boundary condition by pinning the rigid
435 body degrees of freedom, performing a steady Newton solve and then
436 releasing the rigid body degrees of freedom.
