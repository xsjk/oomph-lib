3\mainpage Demo problem: Flow in a 2D channel with an oscillating wall
4
5The moving-boundary Navier-Stokes problem discussed in this document
6is a "warm-up" problem for the classical fluid-structure interaction
7problem of flow in a 2D collapsible channel. Here we compute the flow
8through a 2D channel in which part of one wall is replaced by a moving
9"membrane" whose motion is prescribed. In
10<A HREF="../../../interaction/fsi_collapsible_channel/html/index.html">
11another example</A>, we will
12demonstrate how easy it is to extend the driver code for the current
13problem to a fluid-structure interaction problem in which the "membrane" is
14represented by an elastic beam that deforms in response to the
15fluid traction.
16
17
18We note that the (FSI version of the) problem considered here
19was analysed in much more detail in
20
21<UL>
22<LI> Jensen, O.E. & Heil, M. (2003) High-frequency self-excited
23oscillations in a collapsible-channel flow.
24<EM>Journal of Fluid Mechanics</EM> <STRONG>481</STRONG>
25235-268.
26<A HREF="http://www.maths.man.ac.uk/~mheil/MATTHIAS/PDF/JensenHeil2003.pdf">(pdf
27 preprint)</A>
28<A HREF="http://www.maths.man.ac.uk/~mheil/MATTHIAS/ABSTRACTS/JensenHeil02.html">(abstract)</A>
29</UL>
30where a detailed discussion (and an asymptotic analysis) of the
31flow-structures described below may be found.

36\section problem The problem
37
38<CENTER>
39<TABLE>
40<TR>
41<TD>
42<CENTER>
43<B>Finite-Reynolds-number flow in a 2D channel with an oscillating wall .</B>
44</CENTER>
45
46The figure below shows a sketch of the problem: Flow is driven by a
47prescribed pressure drop through a 2D channel of width
48\f$ H^* \f$ and total length
49\f$ L^*_{total} = L^*_{up} + L^*_{collapsible} + L^*_{down}. \f$
50The upstream and downstream lengths of the channel are rigid, whereas
51the upper wall in the central section performs a prescribed
52oscillation. The shape of the moving segment is parametrised by a Lagrangian
53coordinate, \f$ \zeta^* \f$ , so that the position vector to
54the moving wall is given by \f$ {\bf R}_w^*(\zeta^*,t^*)\f$ .
55
56
57\image html collapsible_channel_sketch.gif "Sketch of the problem. "
58\image latex collapsible_channel_sketch.eps "Sketch of the problem. " width=0.75\textwidth
59
60We scale all lengths on the channel width, \f$ H^* \f$ , use the
61average velocity through the undeformed channel,
62\f$ U =P^*_{up} H^{*2}/(12 \mu L^*_{total}) \f$ ,
63to scale the velocities, and use \f$ H^{*}/U \f$ to non-dimensionalise
64time. (As usual, we employ asterisks distinguish dimensional parameters from
65their non-dimensional equivalents.)
66
67The flow is then governed by the unsteady Navier-Stokes equations
68\f[
69Re\left(St\frac{\partial u_i}{\partial t} +
70u_j\frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_j}\right) =
71- \frac{\partial p}{\partial x_i} +
72\frac{\partial }{\partial x_j} \left(
73\frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_j} +
74\frac{\partial u_j}{\partial x_i} \right)
75 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (1)
76\f]
77and the continuity equation
78\f[
79\frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_i} = 0
80 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (2)
81\f]
82with \f$ St=1 \f$ , subject to the following boundary and initial
83conditions:
84- Initial condition: Poiseuille flow, i.e.
85 \f[
86 {\bf u}(x_1,x_2,t=0) = {\bf u}_{Poiseuille}(x_1,x_2) =
87 6 \ x_2 \ (1-x_2) \ {\bf e}_1.
88 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (3)
89 \f]
90
91- Parallel inflow, \f$ {\bf u} \cdot {\bf e}_2 = {\bf 0}, \f$
92 and an applied axial traction of \f$ {\bf t} \cdot {\bf e}_1 =
93 p_{up} = 12 \ L_{total} \f$ at the upstream end, \f$ x_1=0 \f$ .
94
95- Parallel, axially traction-free outflow at the downstream end, i.e.
96 \f$ {\bf u} \cdot {\bf e}_2 = {\bf 0} \f$ and
97 \f$ {\bf t} \cdot {\bf e}_1 = p_{down} = 0 \f$ at \f$
98 x_1=L_{total} \f$ .
99
100- No slip on all channel walls, i.e. \f$ {\bf u} = {\bf 0} \f$ on
101 the rigid walls and
102 \f[
103 {\bf u} = \frac{\partial {\bf R}_w}{\partial t}
104 \mbox{\ \ \ \ on the moving wall.}
105 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (4)
106 \f]
107
108</TD>
109</TR>
110</TABLE>
111</CENTER>
112
113We consider a wall motion that deforms the initially "flush" wall into
114a parabolic shape. We denote the non-dimensional
115amplitude of the oscillation by \f$ A \f$ and its period by \f$ T \f$ ,
116and parametrise the position vector to a point on the wall by
117the Lagrangian coordinate \f$ \zeta \in [0,L_{collapsible}] \f$ as
118\f[ {\bf R}_w(\zeta,t) =
119\left(
120\begin{array}{l}
121L_{up} + \zeta \\
1221 + A \left(\frac{2}{L_{collapsible}}\right)^2 \zeta \ (L-\zeta)
123 \ \sin(2\pi t / T)
124 \ {\cal R}(t)
125\end{array}
126\right),
127 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (5)
128\f]
129where the "ramp" function
130\f[
131{\cal R}(t) = \left\{
132\begin{array}{ll}
133\frac{1}{2}(1-\cos(\pi t/T)) & \mbox{for $t<T$} \\
1341 & \mbox{for $t \ge T$}
135\end{array}
136\right.
137\f]
138 is used
139to facilitate the start-up of the simulation from
140the initial condition of steady Poiseuille flow. \f$ {\cal R}(t) \f$
141provides a "smooth" startup of the wall motion during the first period
142of the oscillation.

150<A HREF="../figures/flow.avi">the animation of the
151computational results</A>. The first four figures show (from top left to bottom
152right) "carpet plots" of the axial and transverse velocities,
153the axial component of the perturbation velocity

156of the figure shows a contour plot of the pressure and a few

158
159
160\image html flow.gif "Snapshot from the animation of the flow field for Re = Re St = 50, T=0.45, A=0.01. "
161\image latex flow.eps "Snapshot from the animation of the flow field for Re = Re St = 50, T=0.45, A=0.01. " width=0.20\textwidth
162
163The figures illustrate the flow structures identified in
164<A HREF="http://www.maths.man.ac.uk/~mheil/MATTHIAS/PDF/JensenHeil2003.pdf">Jensen \& Heil's (2003)</A> asymptotic analysis of 2D channel flows
165that are driven by high-frequency, small-amplitude oscillations of
166a finite section of one of their walls: The flow consists of oscillatory
167axial "sloshing flows", superimposed on the mean Poiseuille
168flow that is driven by the applied pressure drop. During phases when
169the wall moves inwards (outwards) the flow generated by the moving
170wall decelerates (accelerates) the flow in the upstream region as the wall
171"injects" fluid into ("sucks" fluid out of) the domain. Conversely,
172in the downstream region the flow generated by the wall
173adds to the pressure-driven mean flow during phases when the wall
174moves inwards. This is shown most clearly in the plot of the
175axial velocity perturbation. In the plots shown above, the wall
176moves outwards and the axial perturbation velocity is positive
177(i.e. in the direction of the pressure-driven mean flow)
178in the upstream region, and negative in the downstream region.
179This is also shown in the time-traces of the velocities
180at two control points in the in- and outflow cross-sections,
181shown in the figure below:
182
183
184\image html trace.gif "Time-trace of the axial velocities at two control points in the upstream and downstream cross-sections, and the vertical position of a control point on the wall. (Re = Re St = 50, T=0.45, A=0.01.) "
185\image latex trace.eps "Time-trace of the axial velocities at two control points in the upstream and downstream cross-sections, and the vertical position of a control point on the wall. (Re = Re St = 50, T=0.45, A=0.01.) " width=0.20\textwidth
186
187
188Finally, we comment that the plot of the perturbation velocities
189confirms the two-layer structure of the sloshing flows predicted
190in the asymptotic analysis.
191The sloshing flow comprises a blunt core flow region in which the flow is
192dominated by inertial effects while thin Stokes layers develop near the wall.
193Within these layers, the fluid viscosity reduces the axial velocity to zero.
194The carpet plot of the pressure shows that the pressure
195distribution is dominated by the variations induced by the oscillatory
196sloshing flows. For a detailed discussion of the flow structure we refer
197to <A HREF="http://www.maths.man.ac.uk/~mheil/MATTHIAS/PDF/JensenHeil2003.pdf">
198Jensen \& Heil (2003).</A>

205\section overview Overview of the driver code
206Overall, the driver code is very similar to codes developed
207for other moving-boundary Navier-Stokes problems, such as the
208driver code used to simulate the <A HREF="../../osc_ellipse/html/index.html">
209flow inside an oscillating ellipse.</A> The present code is slightly
210lengthier because of the traction boundary conditions
211which we impose by attaching traction elements to the upstream end of
212the mesh. (Refer to the
213<A HREF="../../../navier_stokes/rayleigh_traction_channel/html/index.html">
214traction-driven Rayleigh problem</A> for a more detailed
215discussion of this technique.) Also, as discussed in
216<A HREF="../../../poisson/two_d_poisson_flux_bc_adapt/html/index.html">
217another example</A>, the traction elements must be removed/re-attached
218before/after every mesh adaptation.
219
220
221The domain is discretised by the
222<A HREF="../../../meshes/mesh_list/html/index.html#collapsible_channel">
223<CODE>CollapsibleChannelMesh</CODE></A>
224which employs the <CODE>CollapsibleChannelDomain</CODE>
225to provide a \c MacroElement - based representation of the
226deforming domain in terms of the \c GeomObject that describes the motion
227of the "collapsible" section of the wall (boundary 3). The sketch
228below illustrates
229the topology and the mesh deformation: As the wall deforms, the
230boundaries of the \c MacroElements in the "collapsible" part
231of the \c Domain follow the wall motion.
232
233
234\image html mesh.gif "Sketch of the CollapsibleChannelDomain/Mesh. "
235\image latex mesh.eps "Sketch of the CollapsibleChannelDomain/Mesh. " width=0.20\textwidth
236
237The no-slip boundary conditions on the moving wall are applied
238as in the <A HREF="../../osc_ellipse/html/index.html">
239oscillating ellipse problem,</A> by executing the function
240\c FSI_functions::apply_no_slip_on_moving_wall(...)
241in \c Problem::actions_before_implicit_timestep() for all
242nodes that are located on boundary 3.
243
244The following sections provide a complete annotated listing of the
245driver code. Most functions should already be familiar from
246previous examples and you may want to skip straight to the
247\ref comments.

317\section main The driver code
318\dontinclude collapsible_channel.cc
319As with most previous time-dependent codes, we use command line
320arguments to indicate if the code is run during \c oomph-lib's
321self-test. If any command line arguments are specified, we
322use a coarser discretisation and perform fewer timesteps.
323
324After storing the command line arguments, we choose the
325number of elements in the mesh, set the lengths of the
326domain and choose the amplitude and period of the oscillation.
327The parameter values are chosen such that the wall motion
328resembles that in the FSI simulations shown in Fig. 5 of
329 <A HREF="http://www.maths.man.ac.uk/~mheil/MATTHIAS/PDF/JensenHeil2003.pdf">
330Jensen \& Heil (2003).</A>
331
332\skipline start_of_driver_code
333\until double period=0.45;
334
335We set the (non-dimensional) upstream pressure to
336\f$ p_{up} = 12 L_{total }\f$ ,
337so that in the absence of any wall oscillation, the steady flow through the
338channel is Poiseuille flow, \f$ {\bf u} = {\bf u}_{Poiseuille} =
3396 \ x_2 \ (1-x_2) \ {\bf e}_1 \f$ ; see \ref comments.
340\until Global_Physical_Variables::P_up=12.0*(lup+lcollapsible+ldown);
341
342Next, we specify the output directory and build the problem with
343refineable 2D Crouzeix Raviart Elements.
344
345\until amplitude,period);
346
347Next we set up the time-stepping parameters for a simulation
348of three periods of the oscillation, performed with 40 timesteps
349per period. Fewer timesteps are performed
350if the code is run in self-test mode.
351
352\until double t_min
353
354We initialise the timestepper and set the initial conditions before
355documenting the initial condition.
356
357\until doc_info.number()++;
358
359
360Next we set the error targets for the adaptive mesh refinement; a
361smaller target error is used when the code is run in self-test mode to ensure
362that some mesh refinement is performed during the first few timesteps.
363
364\until }
365
366
367The timestepping loop itself is identical to that used in
368<A HREF="../../../unsteady_heat/two_d_unsteady_heat_adapt/html/index.html">
369other time-dependent driver codes with adaptive mesh refinement</A>.
370During the first timestep, an arbitrary number of spatial adaptations
371may be performed, as the initial condition can be re-assigned
372on the refined mesh. (This is indicated by setting the boolean flag
373\c first to \c true when calling the spatially adaptive,
374unsteady Newton solver.) During subsequent timesteps the need to interpolate
375the history values onto the refined mesh limits the benefits of
376repeated mesh adaptations and we limit the number of spatial
377adaptations per timestep to 1.
378
379\until end of driver code

597\section comments Comments and Exercises
598-# Check the non-dimensionalisation of the governing
599 equations and confirm that a (non-dimensional) upstream pressure
600 \f$ p_{up} = 12 \ L_{total} \f$ is required to drive the steady
601 Poiseuille flow specified by (3) through the
602 static, undeformed channel. Use this to "validate" (well,
603 "plausibility-check", anyway...) the code by setting the
604 amplitude of the wall oscillation to zero.
605-# Double the upstream pressure while keeping the amplitude
606 of the wall oscillation at zero and confirm that the flow
607 accelerates until it (asymptotically) approaches Poiseuille
608 flow with twice the initial flowrate as \f$ t \to \infty. \f$
609-# The flow field has the largest velocity gradients in the thin
610 Stokes layers near the wall, causing the automatic mesh adaptation
611 procedure to refine the mesh pre-dominantly in these regions.
612 To facilitate the resolution of such layers the
613 \c CollapsibleChannelDomain and \c CollapsibleChannelMesh
614 allow the specification of a mapping [0,1] -> [0,1] that
615 redistributes the nodal points in the vertical direction
616 so that the elements near the wall become more squashed.
617 By default the "boundary-layer squash function" is the identity
618 but it may be overloaded by specifying a function pointer to an
619 alternative function. The driver code already includes a demonstration of
620 this capability which may be activated by compiling the driver
621 code with \c -DUSE_BL_SQUASH_FCT. This activates the code
622 segment
623 \dontinclude collapsible_channel.cc
624 \skipline ifdef USE
625 \until endif
626 in the Problem constructor. The "squash function" used for this
627 example is defined in the following namespace:
628 \dontinclude collapsible_channel.cc
629 \skipline start_of_BL_Squash
630 \until end of BL_Squash
631 With this function 50% of the nodal points in the vertical
632 direction are located within two boundary-layer regions which
633 occupy 2 x 10% of the channel's width. The figure below
