3\mainpage Demo problem: Large-displacement post-buckling of a pressure-loaded thin-walled elastic ring -- displacement-control techniques
4
5In this example we study a more challenging beam problem: The
6non-axisymmetric buckling of a thin-walled elastic
7ring, loaded by a spatially-constant external pressure, \f$ p_{ext}\f$ .
8For sufficiently small positive (or arbitrarily large negative)
9values of \f$ p_{ext}\f$ the ring deforms axisymmetrically and in
10this mode it is very stiff, implying that large changes in pressure
11are required to change its radius. However, if \f$ p_{ext}\f$ exceeds
12a critical threshold, the axisymmetric configuration becomes unstable,
13causing the ring to buckle non-axisymmetrically. Once buckled,
14the ring is much more flexible and small changes in \f$
15p_{ext}\f$ are sufficient to induce dramatic changes in its shape.
16
17The rapid change in stiffness following the buckling makes it
18difficult to compute strongly buckled solutions by continuation
19methods as the solution computed for a certain value of \f$ p_{ext}\f$
20may represent a poor approximation of the solution at a
21slightly larger pressure. In extreme cases, this can
22cause the Newton method (whose convergence relies on the provision of
23good initial guesses for the unknowns) to diverge.
24
25We will demonstrate the use of so-called "displacement control" techniques
26to overcome these problems. Displacement-control techniques are
27useful in problems in which some \e a-priori knowledge about the
28expected displacement field allows us to re-formulate the
29problem. Rather than prescribing the load on the elastic solid
30and computing the displacement field, we prescribe the displacement
31of a carefully-selected material "control" point and regard the
32load required to achieve this displacement as an unknown.

40<B>Non-axisymmetric buckling of a pressure-loaded, thin-walled elastic ring</B>

43We wish to compute the deformation of a linearly-elastic, circular
44ring of undeformed radius \f$ R_0 \f$ and wall thickness \f$ h^* \f$,
45subject to a spatially-constant external pressure \f$ p_{ext}^* \f$.
46We choose the undeformed radius \f$ R_0 \f$ as the lengthscale
47for the non-dimensionalisation of the problem and assume that
48\f$ h = h^*/R_0 \ll 1 \f$ , justifying the use of beam theory.

50the previous example</A> we scale the pressure on the ring's
51effective Young's modulus, \f$ p_{ext} = p_{ext}^*/E_{eff} \f$ ,
52where \f$ E_{eff} = E/(1-\nu^2) \f$ . We use the non-dimensional
53arclength \f$ \xi \in [0,2\pi] \f$ along the ring's undeformed
54centreline as the Lagrangian coordinate and parametrise the position vector
55to the ring's undeformed centreline as
56\f[
57{\bf r}_w(\xi) = \left(
58\begin{array}{c}
59\cos(\xi) \\
60\sin(\xi)
61\end{array}
62\right).
63\f]
64We wish to compute the position vector \f$ {\bf R}_w(\xi) \f$
65to the deformed ring's centreline.

83\section theory The expected deformation and the displacement-control formulation of the problem
84Standard linear stability analysis [see,
85e.g., G.J. Simitses "An introduction to the elastic stability of
86structures", Prentice-Hall, (1976)] predicts the ring to become
87unstable to non-axisymmetric perturbations with an azimuthal
88wavenumber of \f$ N=2 \f$ at a pressure
89of
90\f[
91p_{ext}^{[buckl]} = 3 \times \frac{1}{12} h^3.
92\f]
93For \f$ p_{ext} > p_{ext}^{[buckl]} \f$ we therefore expect the ring to
94deform into a shape similar to the one indicated by the dashed line
95in the above sketch. As the ring buckles non-axisymmetrically,
96the material point on
97the vertical symmetry line (i.e. the material point with Lagrangian coordinate
98\f$ \xi=\pi/2\f$ ) moves radially inwards. This makes it
99an excellent control point for this problem as we can "sweep" through
100the entire range of the ring's post-buckling deformation by
101varying its \f$ x_2 \f$ -- coordinate
102from \f$ R_{ctrl} =1 \f$ (corresponding to the undeformed,
103axisymmetric configuration) to \f$ R_{ctrl} = 0 \f$
104(corresponding to a configuration in which the ring is collapsed
105to the point of opposite wall contact). We note that Flaherty,
106Keller & Rubinow's analysis [SIAM J. Appl. Math \b 23, 446--455
107(1972)], based on an inextensible beam model, predicts opposite
108wall contact to occur at an external pressure of
109\f[
110p_{ext}^{[owc]} = 5.22 \times \frac{1}{12} h^3.
111\f]
112

114the problem from

164\section results Results
165
166The figure below shows computed ring shapes for \f$ h^*/R_0 =
1671/20 \f$. They were obtained in two phases: During the first phase
168of the computation we subjected the ring to a load of the form
169\f[
170{\bf f} = \bigg( p_{ext} - p_{cos} \cos(2 \xi) \bigg) \, {\bf N}
171\f]
172where \f$ {\bf N} \f$ is the outer unit normal on the deformed ring
173and \f$ p_{cos} \ll 1 \f$ is a small cosinusoidal perturbation
174to the external pressure which forces the ring to buckle
175"in the right direction". The undeformed configuration was
176used as the initial guess for an initial computation with
177\f$ p_{ext} = 0\f$ [or, in the case of displacement control,
178\f$ R_{ctrl} = 1\f$ ]. Subsequently we increased \f$ p_{ext} \f$
179[or decreased \f$ R_{ctrl}\f$ ] in small steps, using the previously
180computed solutions for the displacement field [and \f$ p_{ext} \f$ ]
181as initial guesses for the unknowns. This procedure was continued until
182the ring was collapsed up to the point of opposite wall contact.
183During the second phase, we set \f$ p_{cos} = 0\f$ and reversed the
184procedure to re-trace the deformation to the axisymmetric state.

223\section displ_control Applying displacement control in oomph-lib
224
225To facilitate the solution of solid mechanics problems with
226displacement-control techniques, \c oomph-lib provides a
227\c DisplacementControlElement, which may be used to
228add the displacement control constraint to the global system of
229equations that is solved by Newton's method. Applying displacement
230control in a solid mechanics problem involves the following
231straightforward steps:
232-# <B>Formulate the solid mechanics problems in its "standard
233 form":</B> \n\n In this formulation the (scalar) load level is
234 prescribed and the displacements are regarded as unknowns. \n\n
235-# <B>Change the representation of the (scalar) load level to allow
236 it to be an unknown in the Problem:</B> \n\n In the original
237 formulation, the load level
238 is likely to have been represented by a double precision number.
239 To allow the load level to be regarded as an unknown, it must be
240 represented by a value in a \c Data object. Currently,
241 \c oomph-lib's \c DisplacementControlElement expects the load level
242 to be the one-and-only value in the load \c Data object. We note
243 that computations with a prescribed load are still possible and
244 simply require pinning of the value that represents the load. \n\n
245-# <B>"Tell" the solid mechanics elements that the load level is a (potential)
246 unknown:</B> \n\n
247 Since the load on the solid mechanics elements affects their
248 residuals, their elemental Jacobian matrices (which contain
249 the derivatives of the entries in the elemental residual vector
250 with respect to \e all unknowns that affect it) must take the
251 dependence on the (potentially) unknown load level into account. This
252 may be achieved by adding the \c Data object that stores the load
253 level to the element's external \c Data (i.e. \c Data whose
254 values \e affect the element's residual vector, but whose values are
255 not determined \e by the element). External \c Data is
256 automatically included in an element's equation numbering
257 procedure. Furthermore, since the elemental Jacobian matrices of
258 \c SolidFiniteElements are generated by finite-differencing,
259 the derivatives of the element's residual vector with respect
260 to the load level are computed automatically. Consequently, the
261 application of displacement control does not require a
262 re-implementation of the solid mechanics elements. \n\n
263-# <B>Identify a material point in the solid that can serve as a
264 "control point":</B> \n \n Ideally, this control point should move
265 monotonically along one of the (Eulerian) coordinate directions as the
266 deformation of the solid body increases. \n\n
267-# <B>Create a \c DisplacementControlElement and add it to the
268 \c Mesh.</B> \n\n
269 The \c DisplacementControlElement adds the displacement constraint
270 to the global system of equations and thus provides the
271 additional equation required to determine the unknown load
272 level. We note that the \c DisplacementControlElement has
273 two constructors: \n\n
274 - The first version expects a pointer to the \c Data object
275 whose one-and-only value contains the unknown load level.
276 This version of the constructor is appropriate in cases where
277 the load \c Data has already been created elsewhere (as described
278 above). \n\n
279 - The second version of the constructor creates the required
280 load \c Data object internally and provides access to it via
281 a pointer-based access function. \n\n
282 .
283 The section \ref global_data provides a more detailed discussion
284 of when to use which version of the constructor.
285 \n\n
286-# <B>Done!</B> \n\n Set the desired value of the control displacement
287 and solve the problem. \n\n
288.
289
290The driver code discussed below illustrates these steps.

488\section further Further comments and Exercises
489\subsection global_data "Internal" and "external" Data in elements and "global" Data in Problems
490
491In the section \ref constructor we encountered two
492different constructors for the \c DisplacementControlElement.
493- The first version (in which the \c Data object that contains the
494 unknown load value is created by the constructor) is most natural
495 for the problem considered here as the load \c Data is
496 created specifically for the purpose of allowing displacement
497 control to be applied. It therefore natural to regard the
498 \c DisplacementControlElement as being "in charge of" the load
499 \c Data , and storing it in the element's "internal Data".
500 (<A HREF="../../../the_data_structure/html/index.html">Recall</A>
501 that once \c Data is stored in an element's internal \c Data,
502 the element becomes responsible for performing tasks such as equation
503 numbering, timestepping, etc. that must be performed exactly once
504 for each \c Data value in the \c Problem.) \n\n
505- The second version of the constructor is appropriate for problems
506 in which the load \c Data has already been created by another
507 element, implying that the other element is already "in charge"
508 of the \c Data object and performs the equation numbering,
509 timestepping etc. for its values. In that case, the load \c Data
510 is regarded as "external Data" for the \c
511 DisplacementControlElement.
512.
513In our example code, we simulated the second scenario
514by creating the load \c Data object before calling the
515second version of the constructor. While this ensures that
516the load \e can be regarded as an unknown in the problem,
517the \c Problem remains "unaware" of the additional unknown,
518as none of the elements in the \c Problem's mesh is "in charge"
519of it. While this is clearly a
520somewhat artificial scenario, \c oomph-lib provides a
521mechanism for handling such cases: Adding a \c Data object
522to the \c Problem's "global Data" by calling
523
524\code
525Problem::add_global_data(...)
526\endcode
527
528puts the \c Problem itself "in charge" of this \c Data.
